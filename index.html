    The Monte-Carlo methodology is perhaps the least useful technique here discussed. It is used commonly enough, though, to demonstrate the applicability of computers to mathematical analysis. It involves the theoretical situation of a dart board having exactly square dimensions in which a circle is inscribed such that the edges of the circle are tangent to the edges of the box at exactly four points and do not cross otherwise. Onto the board a colossal number of darts are dropped at random coordinates, the probability that a dart will fall into the circular portion of the board can then be calculated and compared to the actual area of the square to give an approximation of the area of the circle which can then be used to approximate pi.

    The Archimedian methodology, like the Monte-Carlo methodology, is not particularly useful and relies upon geometrical intricacies to make it's point. However, it is somewhat more elegant than the Monte-Carlo methodology which, while fascinating, amounts to little more than a brute force attack on the problem. The Archimedian method involves sandwiching a circle in the bounds of two polygons with calculable areas and therefore concluding that the circle's area must be less than the outer but greater than the inner.
